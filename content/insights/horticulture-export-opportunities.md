Zimbabwe's horticulture sector has quietly rebuilt itself over the last decade. The opportunity in front of growers today — particularly those willing to invest in compliance, packaging and consistent quality — is meaningful.

## Which crops are moving

Three categories consistently outperform on export markets:

- **Sugar snap peas and mangetout** — premium European retailers continue to source these year-round, and Zimbabwean winter production fills a critical seasonal gap
- **Baby vegetables** — courgettes, carrots, leeks and the "baby" cuts of common European staples command strong margins
- **Fresh herbs** — mint, basil, coriander and chives travel well and have steady demand

## What buyers actually want

It's tempting to assume that the limiting factor for export is volume. In our experience it's almost always **consistency** — consistent grade, consistent supply schedule, consistent packaging. A buyer in Rotterdam needs to know that what arrives in week 12 is the same as what arrived in week 8.

The growers who do best treat export as a discipline, not a destination. Cold chain, traceability paperwork, GlobalG.A.P. certification — these aren't paperwork burdens, they're the price of entry.

## Where the local market still wins

Not every grower needs to chase exports. The domestic and regional fresh produce trade is healthier than it has been in years. Urban retail, restaurants and informal traders all want reliable supply of leafy greens, tomatoes, onions and seasonal vegetables.

For growers building their footprint, our advice is consistent:

- Start with a domestic anchor customer who pays on time
- Layer regional trade once the operation is humming
- Only chase export markets when you can guarantee scheduled, graded supply
